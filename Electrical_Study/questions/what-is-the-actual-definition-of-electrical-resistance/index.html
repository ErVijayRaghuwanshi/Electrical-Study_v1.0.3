What is the actual definition of electrical resistance?

When current flows through a conductor it face some opposition or hindrance. This property of conductor or any other matter to oppose the flow of current within itself can be termed as resistance. The conventional definition or mathematical representation of resistance can be given as the ratio of voltage supplied and the current flow, i.e. R = V/I.

Now for a good conductor, the value of R is less as for good current flow the opposition has to be as low as possible. Now this property also differentiate the matters into the parts conductor, insulator and semiconductors. It can be easily said that for conductor, resistance is very less, for semiconductors it is a little more and for insulators the resistance is very high. That is why there is almost no current flow through insulator. Ideally for conductors, the resistance should be zero and for insulators this resistance should be infinity, although practically these zero and infinity resistances are not possible.

Another specific definition of resistance is there, which is R = ρ( l/A )

This is mainly used to determine the resistance of wires. Here ρ is the material's specific resistivity, l is the length of the wire, and A is the cross-section of the wire and R is the calculated resistance of the wire. For different materials, i.e for copper, aluminium the resistivity is different which results in the different resistance of different wires of same length and cross-section. So, it can be said that resistance is a very important parameter in electrical engineering.
