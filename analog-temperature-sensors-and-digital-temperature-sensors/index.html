Digital Temperature Sensor

This is a type of solid state temperature sensors that gives output in digital format. The rise of temperature microcontroller based systems and the development of various communication protocols have led to the ultimate design of these types of temperature sensors.

A normal analog temperature sensor gives the output in the form of time changing voltage which varies depending on the surrounding temperature. In the digital temperature sensors, the output is not a varying analog voltage. But the signal is sent to the microcontroller via a communication protocol that is available in both the controller and sensor. I2C (Inter IC Communication) and SPI (Serial Peripheral Interface) are two such protocols most widely used in the digital temperature sensors.

The two most widely used digital sensors are the TMP100 from Texas Instruments and DS18B20 from Maxim Integrated. Let us discuss each of them in brief here.

TMP100

The temperature range of TMP100 extends from - 55 to 125 degree Celsius. It operates on the I2C protocol and up to 8 devices can be connected on the bus. TMP100 offers a resolution of 0.0625 degree Celsius. The resolution is user selectable and can be 9 to 12 bits. The voltage supply range is from 2.7V to 5.5V.

How to use TMP100?

Consider that we are using only one sensor IC on the Bus. So we ground both the Address pins ADD0 and ADD1. If more than one sensor IC is to be used than a different configuration of ADD0 and ADD1 may be required. This will give a slave address of 1001000 to the sensor. So, whenever the master microcontroller wants to communicate with the sensor, we have to use this address. In order to initiate the conversion process, we have to write to appropriate data into the internal pointer register of the sensor. This can be done by first writing the address of the sensor on the bus and then sending the data.

The two LSB bits into the pointer register is used to set from which register the next read or write operation has to be performed. There are 4 registers in a TMP100 viz. Temperature register, Configuration register, TLow and Thigh registers.

Temperature Register - It stores the 12 bit value of the most recent conversion. It is in the form of two 8 bit registers and two successive read operations are performed to get the complete data.

Configuration Register - It is used to set the resolution (9 to 12 bits) and also to program the device in single conversion mode.

TLow and Thigh-These two registers are used to implement a very interesting function. We can program the configuration registers and these two registers in such a way that when the temperature exceeds a set value in the Thigh register, an alert signal goes high until the temperature becomes lower than the TLow register value. However, this feature is available only in TMP101.

Analog Temperature Sensor

These measure the ambient temperature and give an output voltage that is proportional to the input voltage. As the ambient temperature increases the voltage output of the IC also increases. Like digital temperature sensors, analog ones are also compact in size and come in variety of temperature ranges right from -55 degrees to around 150 degree Celsius. Also, they are available in a voltage supply range from 1.8V to 5.3V.

Many semiconductor manufacturers make analog temperature sensors and each has its own temperature and supply voltage range. The LM35 series of sensor from Texas Instruments is a widely used and most popular analog temperature IC which is implemented in a large number of projects. Let us discuss it here.

The analog output is given to the microcontroller which uses its internal ADC to convert the voltage to a digital value.The ADC reference voltage is important here.If we use the sensor in basic range from 0⁰ to 100⁰C, then the maximum voltage will be 100*10mV = 1V. So a ADC reference of say 1.5 volts can be used.
Analog V/S Digital Sensors

The decision whether to use a analog or a digital sensor in an application depends entirely on the requirements of the design and the level of accuracy that is wanted.

A digital sensor require that a specialized communication protocol is present in the controller.This may make the project a little expensive, but the resolution of the sensor can now be programmed by the user. Also digital sensors have facilities like range bound alert signalling and the set point can be stored in a non volatile memory. Also the programming becomes a little lengthy and complex.When a large number of sensors are interfaced to a single system, digital sensors come in handy.

An analog sensor on the other hand is easy on pocket and also the accuracy and resolution provided by it is sufficient for a not so critical application. Also a controller with an ADC is only required.The programming also becomes easy.
