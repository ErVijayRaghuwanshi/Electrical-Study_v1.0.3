What does the thevenin’s theorem state

In circuit analysis, Thévenin's theorem for linear electrical network states that any combination of voltage sources, current sources, and resistors with two terminals may be considered as electrically equivalent to a single voltage source V or a single current source I or a single series resistor R.

Getting confused? Let’s make it simpler:

At the beginning to study of this particular theorem it should be mentioned that this theorem is very much conceptual as well as easy too.

Suppose we have to calculate the current through any branch in a circuit & this branch is connected with the circuit by its two terminals. As because there are active sources present in the circuit so there should be a potential difference in between the terminals of the branch & by this potential difference a current is passing through the branch. So, rest of the circuit may be assumed as a single voltage source, whose voltage is the open circuit voltage between the terminals of the said branch & the internal resistance of the circuit is nothing but the equivalent resistance of the circuit looking from into the terminals towards the circuit.

So theorem can also be stated as:

• When a particular branch is removed from a circuit, the open circuit voltage appears across the terminals of that circuit, called Thevenin's equivalent voltage.

• The equivalent resistance of the circuit looking from the terminal towards the circuit ,is called Thevenin's equivalent resistance.

• Ultimately when we replace the rest of the circuit by a single voltage source, then the voltage of the source would be Thevenin's equivalent voltage and internal resistance of the voltage source would be Thevenin's equivalent resistance which would be connected in series.
