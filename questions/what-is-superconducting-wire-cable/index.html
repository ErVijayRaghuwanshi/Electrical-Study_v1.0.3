What is Superconducting Wire or Cable?

Superconducting cable or wires are made up of superconductors which exhibits superconductivity. Common superconductors in use are niobium - titanium and pure mercury. Superconducting wires or cables show superconductivity keeping three parameters or conditions in mind. These are â€“ current density, temperature and magnetic field. Superconducting wire is preferred over aluminum or copper wire owing to its high current density and almost zero power dissipation. High temperature superconducting cable (HTS) first came in the market in 1986 and it is still in the development phase. Superconductor cables have zero electrical resistance thus power loss during transmission is almost negligible.

Superconducting wires are used for ratings up to 4000 amps. HTS materials are to kept at very low temperatures to maintain superconducting characteristics. Cryogens like liquid nitrogen or helium are used to keep superconducting temperatures. Disadvantages of superconductivity includes high cost of maintaining materials at superconducting temperatures, sometimes poor mechanical strength of the wires, costly materials and complicated construction, etc. Superconducting cables consists of strands which is made up of filaments. To cover 7600 km of cable around 1200 tonnes of HTC are required. Each cable is made up of 36 strands of superconducting wires. Each strand has 6300 filaments in it. Thickness and diameter of each filament and strand are 0.006 mm and 0.825 mm respectively.
