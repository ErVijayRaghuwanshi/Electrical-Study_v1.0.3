What is Seebeck effect ?

If two dissimilar metals are connected end to end, forming two different junctions and a temperature difference is maintained between these junctions, a potential difference generates between the junctions, this phenomenon is known as the Seebeck effect.

The father of this effect, Thomas Johann Seebeck, in 1821 discovered that a compass needle was deflected when he connected two metals to form a closed loop and provided a temperature difference between the junctions. He failed to understand the fact that an electric current was produced in the loop, which was the main cause of the magnetic field, and so named this phenomenon as the thermomagnetic effect i.e something that relates thermo (Temperature) with magnetic (magnetism).

What happens is, every metal by the virtue of its properties reacts differently to the same temperature difference, and hence each metal induces a different voltage for same conditions, this creates a potential difference between the two junctions, which in turn creates a current in the loop and hence a magnetic field.

Thermocouples are an example where Seebeck effect is employed.

Things to note:

  1. If the two metals used to observe Seebeck effect are same or had the same Seebeck coefficient then no emf would be generated.
  2. If a larger temperature difference is maintained between the two junctions then the generated emf would also be greater.
  3. If the temperature difference between junctions is not significant then the generated emf will be insignificant and hence not observable.
